Current slide plan:

1. By The Time You Graduate
    Hook:
        Today, for the price of two bubble teas a month, you can rent an intelligence that can write, explain, argue, critique, code, and create. It can help with essays, calculus, programming, politics, and art. It never needs sleep, motivation, or a lunch break. It is not always right, but it is always available, always fast, and always ready to try again.
    Supporting points:
        a. That kind of tool changes what "normal effort" looks like.
        b. It changes what one student can get done in a day.
        c. And it changes what the world may quietly start expecting from you.
    Graphic:
        a. A student desk at night with notes, laptop, bubble tea cups, and a ghostly AI presence woven through the scene like an invisible extra worker.

2. The Question
    Hook:
        If AI can do the task faster, what are you still in school to learn?
    Supporting points:
        a. The car goes faster. We still run.
        b. Better tools do not remove human effort; they change what human effort is for.
        c. Learning is still how you build judgment, taste, and control.
    Graphic:
        a. Split image of a sports car beside a runner on a track.

3. AI Is Already Here
    Hook:
        AI can already help with writing, revision, code, images, and planning.
    Supporting points:
        a. This is not a future demo anymore.
        b. Many students are already using it quietly.
        c. The baseline for what one person can produce is moving.
    Graphic:
        a. Collage of AI outputs on one desk: essay feedback, code editor, generated image, spreadsheet, and notes.

4. Your Timing Matters
    Hook:
        You are entering university and work just as the rules start to shift.
    Supporting points:
        a. Older students had a more stable playbook.
        b. Your batch will likely be asked to work differently.
        c. That is risky, but it is also an opportunity.
    Graphic:
        a. A road splitting toward "University" and "Work", both disappearing into fog or neon haze.

5. Do Not Wait for Permission
    Hook:
        Schools, universities, and companies are still figuring out what "using AI well" even means.
    Supporting points:
        a. Even institutions are experimenting.
        b. In many fields, nobody can clearly describe the workflow three years from now.
        c. Waiting for an official syllabus is too slow.
    Graphic:
        a. A classroom or lecture hall overlaid with loading bars, revision marks, or "updating..." motifs.

6. The Real Competition
    Hook:
        AI is not the only thing competing with you. The person using AI well is.
    Supporting points:
        a. Same person, same task, different tools.
        b. One version struggles alone.
        c. The other uses AI as tutor, editor, and assistant.
    Graphic:
        a. Split screen of the same student doing the same assignment twice, one overwhelmed and one augmented by AI tools.

7. How To Work With AI
    Hook:
        Treat AI as a tutor, sparring partner, intern, and automator.
    Supporting points:
        a. Tutor: explain things at your level.
        b. Sparring partner: challenge your argument and point out gaps.
        c. Intern: draft version 1 quickly.
        d. Automator: handle repetitive tasks.
    Graphic:
        a. Four bold cards or panels, each showing one role with a simple icon and an example task.

8. Start Experimenting Now
    Hook:
        The fastest way to learn is to try things before you need them badly.
    Supporting points:
        a. Open an account and use it regularly.
        b. Make it teach you, critique your work, and generate ideas.
        c. Try code, art, music, notes, or revision prompts.
        d. See what breaks, then learn how to recover.
    Graphic:
        a. A challenge board or checklist styled like a 7-day sprint, with tasks getting ticked off.

9. Use It, Do Not Surrender To It
    Hook:
        AI is powerful, but your job is still to verify, decide, and take responsibility.
    Supporting points:
        a. It can be wrong.
        b. It can sound confident while being wrong.
        c. If you stop thinking, you lose the real benefit.
    Graphic:
        a. Human hand on the steering wheel while an AI dashboard suggests routes and actions.

10. Closing Challenge
    Hook:
        Learn to work with AI before the world quietly starts expecting you to.
    Supporting points:
        a. Start early.
        b. Build instincts.
        c. Arrive at university already experimenting.
    Graphic:
        a. A lunch tray on a conveyor belt moving away while one student reaches early and another reacts too late.
